About Khadi

Khadi, a hand-spun, handwoven fabric, holds a significant place in India's history, culture, and identity. Popularized by Mahatma Gandhi during the struggle for independence, it symbolized self-reliance and resistance against British colonial rule. Through the Swadeshi movement, Gandhi encouraged Indians to spin and weave their own clothes, uniting people across social and economic boundaries in a shared vision of national pride and economic independence. Post-independence, khadi retained its legacy of simplicity and sustainability, aligning with contemporary values of environmental consciousness. Today, it transcends its political roots, celebrated in both traditional attire and modern fashion, representing India’s commitment to preserving indigenous crafts while supporting local artisans and sustainable innovation.

Khadi Heritage Collection

Mahatma Gandhi’s Khadi Dhoti

Date: 1921, during the Non-Cooperation Movement

Location: Worn throughout India, most prominently in Ahmedabad and at public gatherings

Symbolism: Gandhi’s handspun khadi dhoti and shawl became a rallying point for self-reliance and resistance to British colonial rule. His attire symbolized a powerful shift toward local craftsmanship, emphasizing simplicity and India's independence from foreign textiles.

Jawaharlal Nehru’s Khadi Jacket

Date: 1952, first popularized after Nehru became Prime Minister of independent India

Location: Delhi, India, worn at diplomatic and public events

Symbolism: The “Nehru Jacket,” with its mandarin collar and sleek design, symbolized modern India’s dignity and self-assurance. As Nehru wore it on global platforms, this jacket represented India’s cultural sophistication and commitment to self-reliance through khadi.

Indira Gandhi’s Khadi Sarees

Date: Circa 1966, when she first became Prime Minister of India

Location: Delhi, India, especially in Parliament and at diplomatic meetings

Symbolism: Indira Gandhi’s plain khadi sarees in muted colors conveyed both strength and humility. Her attire resonated with a message of resilience and solidarity with India’s heritage, emphasizing her connection to Indian values amid political responsibility.

Vaishali S’s Bisra Collection

Date: August 2023, launched during Lakmé Fashion Week

Location: Mumbai, India

Symbolism: Vaishali S’s Bisra Collection celebrates sustainable fashion, blending traditional Indian craftsmanship with modern design. Using handwoven fabrics, khadi, and natural dyes, it highlights eco-consciousness and a return to nature. The collection’s name, Bisra, symbolizes purity and a deep connection to the earth, reflecting the designer’s commitment to sustainable luxury and India’s artisanal heritage.

Sabyasachi’s Khadi Bridal Sarees

Date: November 2019, at a bridal collection launch in Mumbai

Location: Mumbai, India

Symbolism: Sabyasachi’s khadi bridal sarees, adorned with zari and jewel tones, reimagined khadi as a fabric for opulent ceremonies. This collection highlighted khadi’s adaptability to traditional wedding attire, celebrating India’s heritage with a modern, luxurious touch.

11.11/Eleven Eleven’s Khadi Denim

Date: January 2021, launched as part of a sustainable streetwear line

Location: Delhi, India

Symbolism: 11.11’s khadi denim collection in indigo highlighted khadi’s eco-friendly appeal for streetwear. The soft, handwoven texture of the denim symbolized a modern interpretation of khadi, emphasizing artisanal craftsmanship in sustainable fashion.

Khadi Denim Project

Date: 2021

Location: Delhi, India

Symbolism: In collaboration with the Khadi and Village Industries Commission (KVIC), Rajesh Pratap Singh’s Khadi Denim Project combines traditional handspun khadi with modern denim, blending India’s textile heritage with contemporary streetwear. This collection underscores khadi’s versatility and eco-friendly ethos, symbolizing a forward-thinking approach to fashion that honors India’s artisanship while resonating with global, modern aesthetics.

Raag by Beej

Date: 2021

Location: Mumbai, India

Symbolism: Beej Asha Sarabhai Raag highlights khadi’s timeless connection to Indian heritage while embracing sustainable, contemporary design. The collection symbolizes khadi's adaptability and resilience, blending traditional craftsmanship with modern aesthetics to celebrate eco-conscious fashion and the enduring legacy of handwoven textiles.

Bodice Summer 2020 Collection

Date: January 2020, as part of the Woolmark Prize

Location: London, United Kingdom

Symbolism: This Bodice dress by Ruchika Sachdeva highlights a minimalist design that honors khadi’s sustainable roots. The clean lines and understated details showcase khadi’s versatility, positioning it as a refined, eco-conscious fabric in modern fashion. Through this collection, Bodice emphasizes khadi’s potential to blend traditional craftsmanship with a contemporary aesthetic, symbolizing a fusion of heritage and sustainability for a global audience.

 

Khadi - A Symbol of Heritage and Sustainability

The Evolution of Khadi in Indian Fashion

Khadi has become a foundational element in Indian fashion, symbolizing both heritage and sustainability. Introduced by Mahatma Gandhi as a call for self-reliance during India’s freedom movement, khadi grew into a fabric embodying simplicity and resilience. In the post-independence era, designers reimagined khadi for modern tastes, creating everything from bridal wear to contemporary street style, showcasing its adaptability. Today, khadi is a blend of tradition and innovation, used by Indian fashion icons to create globally admired, eco-conscious designs. It remains a proud emblem of India’s artisanal legacy and its commitment to sustainable fashion practices.
